The moment a client signs is your first real chance to show them what working with you is like.

If that moment is followed by silence — no welcome email, no clear next steps, no communication for a day or two while you scramble to set things up — you've already created doubt. They're wondering if they made the right choice.

Now imagine the opposite: they sign the contract, and within 30 seconds they have a warm welcome email in their inbox, a link to book the kickoff call, an intake form to gather what you need, and a clear sense of what happens next. You didn't do any of that manually. It all ran automatically.

That's what this post teaches you to build.

---

## What Manual Onboarding Actually Looks Like

Be honest with yourself for a moment. Here's what most agency onboarding looks like in practice:

- Contract signed on Monday
- Welcome email sent… Wednesday (because you were in meetings Tuesday)
- Project created in ClickUp… Thursday (because you forgot Wednesday)
- Team notified via Slack… Friday (a one-liner, no context)
- Intake form sent… the following Monday (you had to remember it existed)
- Kickoff call scheduled… a week after that (back-and-forth over email)

Each delay sends a signal. Not intentionally — but it does. And by the time the kickoff call finally happens, the initial excitement has cooled.

The good news: every single one of those steps can be triggered automatically, instantly, and consistently — every time a new client signs.

## How It Works: Step by Step

### Step 1: Map Out Your Ideal Onboarding Sequence

Before touching any tool, write down every single thing that *should* happen when a new client comes on board. Be thorough. Include:

- What the client needs to *receive* (emails, links, forms)
- What your team needs to *do* (project setup, task assignments, notifications)
- What information you need to *collect* (intake form responses)
- What needs to be *filed or stored* (contract copies, client details in your CRM)

A typical sequence looks like this:

| Step | What Happens | Who/What Does It |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Welcome email + kickoff booking link | Automation → Client |
| 2 | Intake form sent | Automation → Client |
| 3 | New project created in PM tool | Automation → ClickUp/Notion |
| 4 | Tasks created + assigned | Automation → Team |
| 5 | Team notified | Automation → Slack |
| 6 | Client added to CRM | Automation → Airtable/HubSpot |
| 7 | Follow-up if intake form not submitted | Automation → Client (Day 3) |

> 📸 *Screenshot: Onboarding checklist in a Google Doc or Notion page — your blueprint before you build*

> ✅ **After this step:** You have a clear picture of everything the automation needs to do. Don't skip this step — it makes the build 10x faster.

### Step 2: Choose Your Trigger

Your trigger is the single event that kicks the entire onboarding sequence off. Pick the most reliable signal that a client is confirmed:

**Contract signed (Recommended)**
If you're using PandaDoc or DocuSign, Make.com can listen for a "Document Signed" webhook. This is the cleanest trigger — it fires the moment both parties have signed.

**Payment received**
If you use Stripe, Make.com can trigger on a successful payment. Useful if you take an upfront deposit before starting.

**Form submitted**
If you have a "ready to start" form that clients fill in after signing, this works well. It also captures useful information you can pass into the rest of the sequence.

> 📸 *Screenshot: Make.com trigger module — showing PandaDoc "Document Signed" webhook as the trigger*

> ⚠️ **Important:** Whatever you choose, make sure the trigger only fires for confirmed clients — not prospects or leads. Test this carefully before going live.

---

### Step 3: Send the Welcome Email

Your first action after the trigger fires is the welcome email. This sets the tone for the entire relationship.

**What to include:**
- A warm, genuine opening (write it in your voice — not "Dear Valued Client")
- A clear statement of what happens next — so they know what to expect
- A link to book the kickoff call via Calendly or Cal.com
- A link to the intake form they need to complete
- Your contact details if they have questions

**What to avoid:**
- Walls of text
- Corporate-speak
- Anything that sounds like it was written by a lawyer

> 📸 *Screenshot: Gmail template editor in Make.com showing the welcome email with personalisation variables*

Here's a simple structure that works:

> *Hi &#123;&#123;first_name&#125;&#125;,*
>
> *Welcome — we're so glad to have you on board. This is the beginning of something good.*
>
> *Here's what happens next:*
> *1. Fill in this short intake form so we can hit the ground running: [link]*
> *2. Book your kickoff call here — pick a time that works for you: [Calendly link]*
>
> *I'll be in touch ahead of the call. If anything comes up before then, reply to this email.*
>
> *Looking forward to it,*
> *[Your Name]*

Short. Clear. Human.

> 💡 **Pro tip:** In Make.com's Gmail module, you can use the client's first name, company name, and project name as variables — pulled directly from the trigger data. It feels personal even though it's automated.

---

### Step 4: Send the Intake Form

Within the same automation (or a few minutes later), send the intake form. Use **Tally** to build a form that captures everything you need before work begins. Common fields include:

- Business goals for this project
- Target audience / key stakeholders
- Brand assets (logo, fonts, colours) — Tally supports file uploads
- Access credentials or tool invites needed
- Key dates or deadlines
- Any important context or background

> 📸 *Screenshot: Tally intake form with example fields, showing file upload option*

> 💡 **Pro tip:** Keep the intake form separate from the welcome email — don't put everything in one email. Welcome first, form second. It's easier to process, and completion rates are higher when you're not overwhelming someone on day one.

---

### Step 5: Create the Project in Your PM Tool

At the same time as the client emails, Make.com creates the project in your project management tool.

**In ClickUp:**
Use the "Create a Folder" or "Create a List" module to create a new project space. Apply your project template using the ClickUp "Create Task" module in a loop — one module call per task in your standard project structure.

**In Notion:**
Use the "Create a Page" module to create a new project page from a template. Set the project name using the client's name from your trigger data.

> 📸 *Screenshot: Make.com module creating a new ClickUp project with the client name set as the project title*

For each task, set:
- **Assignee** — mapped to a team member (you can use a fixed value or make it dynamic)
- **Due date** — calculated as X days from the project start date using Make.com's date functions
- **Priority** — based on your standard project setup

> ✅ **After this step:** A fully structured project exists in your PM tool before the kickoff call even happens. Your team can see it, prepare for it, and hit the ground running.

---

### Step 6: Notify Your Team in Slack

Once the project is created, send an automatic Slack message to your team channel (or the relevant people) with:

- Client name and company
- Project name
- Kickoff call date (if it's been booked)
- Link to the project in ClickUp or Notion

> 📸 *Screenshot: Slack notification sent via Make.com showing client name, project link, and next steps*

> 💡 **Pro tip:** Include a direct link to the intake form response in the Slack message so your team can review it before the kickoff call — without asking you to forward it.

---

### Step 7: Set a Follow-Up Reminder for the Intake Form

Not every client submits the intake form right away. Build in a contingency: if the form hasn't been submitted within 3 days, Make.com sends a gentle nudge automatically.

To set this up:
1. After sending the intake form, Make.com writes the submission time to a field in Airtable (or a Google Sheet)
2. A separate scheduled scenario runs daily and checks for clients where "Intake Form Submitted = No" and "Days Since Onboarding > 3"
3. If found, it sends a short follow-up email: *"Hi &#123;&#123;first_name&#125;&#125;, just a quick reminder to fill in the intake form before our kickoff call — it really helps us prepare. Here's the link: [link]"*

> 📸 *Screenshot: Make.com scheduled scenario checking for unsubmitted intake forms and sending follow-up emails*

> ✅ **After this step:** You never have to chase a client for their intake form manually again.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Trying to automate the entire onboarding before you've standardised it.**
If your current onboarding is different for every client, automating it will just automate the chaos. Standardise first — decide what *always* happens — then automate that.

**Making the welcome email feel like it came from a system.**
The welcome email is a relationship moment. Read it out loud. If it sounds robotic, rewrite it. Automation handles the delivery, not the writing.

**Not having a project template ready before building the Make.com scenario.**
Your PM tool needs a template to copy from. If that template doesn't exist, Make.com has nothing to work with. Build the template first, test it manually, then automate it.

**Sending everything in one big email.**
Welcome email, intake form, kickoff booking, project details, terms — all in one message is overwhelming. Break it into at least two touchpoints: welcome first, intake form second.

**Not testing with a real email address before going live.**
Always send a test run to yourself before the automation touches a real client. Check every email, every link, every form, every Slack message. This takes 10 minutes and saves enormous embarrassment.
