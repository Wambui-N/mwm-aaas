*"Does Tuesday work? Actually Wednesday is better — how about 2pm? I have something at 2, maybe 3? Can we do Thursday instead?"*

If you've typed any version of this email in the past month, you've wasted time that should never have been yours to waste.

Scheduling a meeting is not a creative task. It's not a strategic one. It's a logistics problem — and logistics problems are exactly what automation is built to solve.

By the end of this post, you'll have a fully automated scheduling system: one link, instant confirmation, automatic reminders, and a post-meeting follow-up that goes out without you thinking about it.

---

## The Hidden Cost of Manual Scheduling

It doesn't feel like much — a few emails, a bit of back-and-forth. But add it up.

A single meeting scheduling exchange takes an average of 8 minutes and 5 email replies. If you're scheduling 5 meetings a week, that's 40 minutes per week, over 30 hours per year — just on *finding* a time to meet. Not the meeting itself. Just the scheduling.

And that's the obvious cost. The hidden cost is the context-switching: each time you break focus to reply to a scheduling email, you lose momentum on whatever you were working on.

One booking link. Zero back-and-forth. That's what this post sets up.

### Step 2: Configure Your Availability

Before sharing your link, set clear rules for when you're bookable:

- **Working hours** — e.g. Mon–Fri, 9am–5pm
- **Buffer time between meetings** — 15–30 minutes recommended
- **Daily meeting limit** — e.g. maximum 3 discovery calls per day
- **Minimum notice period** — e.g. no meetings booked less than 24 hours in advance
- **Days off** — block your deep work days or no-meeting Fridays

These settings protect your calendar from becoming a free-for-all.

> 📸 *Screenshot: Calendly availability settings panel showing working hours, buffers, and booking limits*

> 💡 **Pro tip:** Block a 2–3 hour "deep work" window every morning before opening up to bookings. Use minimum notice of 24 hours so you always have time to prepare. These two settings alone make a significant difference to how your days feel.

> ⚠️ **Don't offer too much availability.** More available slots doesn't mean better service — it means more context-switching. Fewer, better-timed calls are more productive than a calendar packed back-to-back.

---

### Step 3: Create Specific Event Types for Different Meeting Purposes

Instead of one generic "book a call" link, create event types that match the purpose:

| Event Type | Duration | Who It's For |
|---|---|---|
| **Discovery Call** | 30 min | New enquiries and leads |
| **Kickoff Call** | 60 min | New clients — first project meeting |
| **Check-In** | 20 min | Existing clients — progress updates |
| **Strategy Session** | 90 min | Paid consulting — deep work |
| **Internal Sync** | 30 min | Team meetings |

Each event type can have:
- Its own duration
- Its own set of intake questions (e.g. *"What's the main thing you want to cover?"*)
- Its own confirmation message
- Its own reminder sequence
- Its own video call link (Zoom, Google Meet, etc.)

> 📸 *Screenshot: Calendly dashboard showing multiple event types listed with durations and descriptions*

> 💡 **Pro tip:** Give each event type a short, specific name and a description. "Discovery Call — 30 min: For new enquiries about automation services" tells the person booking exactly what to expect. Clarity reduces no-shows.

---

### Step 4: Customise Confirmations and Reminders

Calendly (and Cal.com) automatically send a confirmation email when someone books, and reminder emails before the meeting. Don't use the defaults — customise them to sound like you.

**Confirmation email should include:**
- A warm welcome / thank-you
- What to expect from the call
- Any preparation they should do (optional)
- The video call link (Zoom / Google Meet)
- How to reschedule if needed

**Reminder emails:**
- **24 hours before:** *"Looking forward to our call tomorrow at [time]. Here's the link: [video call link]"*
- **1 hour before:** *"Just a reminder we're on in an hour. See you soon: [link]"*

> 📸 *Screenshot: Calendly email notification settings panel with confirmation and two reminder toggles enabled*

> 💡 **Pro tip:** For discovery calls, add a question in the booking form: *"What's the one thing you most want to get out of this call?"* This makes the call more productive and shows the person that you've read their answer before joining.

### Step 6: Automate the Post-Meeting Follow-Up

This is one of the most underused automations in client-facing businesses. After the meeting ends, you want to:

- Send a "great to meet you" email with a summary of next steps
- Move the lead to the next stage in your tracker
- Create a follow-up task in your PM tool

Use Make.com to trigger this sequence based on the meeting end time:

1. **Schedule a delayed action** — set Make.com to wait until 30 minutes after the meeting end time
2. **Send the follow-up email** — with a personalised message referencing the meeting topic
3. **Update the Airtable record** — move status from "Discovery Call Scheduled" to "Proposal Needed" (or whatever fits your pipeline)
4. **Create a task in ClickUp** — "Follow up with &#123;&#123;client_name&#125;&#125; on proposal"

> 📸 *Screenshot: Make.com delay module set to 30 minutes after meeting end time, followed by a Gmail module*

Here's a simple follow-up email template:

>

> 💡 **Pro tip:** You can write a generic version of this in Make.com and then personalise it manually before it sends — using the same "review before sending" step from the proposal automation. Or, if your calls follow a consistent structure, the generic version is often good enough.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Not customising the confirmation email.**
The default Calendly confirmation is fine functionally, but impersonal. Rewrite it in your voice. Add something useful — an article to read before the call, a short agenda. It takes 10 minutes and makes a real difference.

**Offering too much availability.**
This is the most common mistake. More available slots doesn't signal enthusiasm — it can signal that you're always available, which reduces perceived value. Be selective about when you're bookable.

**Not adding buffer time between meetings.**
Without buffer time, you'll end up in back-to-back calls with no time to take notes, breathe, or prepare for the next one. Even 15 minutes between calls makes the day feel completely different.

**Forgetting to set a minimum notice period.**
Without a minimum notice period, someone can book a meeting 10 minutes from now. Set at least 24 hours (ideally 48) so you always have time to prepare.

**Not following up after the call.**
The follow-up email is where deals often move forward or stall. Most people don't send one because they forget. Automating it means it always goes — and it always goes at the right time.
